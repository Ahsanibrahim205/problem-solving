The steps of problem solving, getting from ‘what if’ to ‘I will’

  • Begin to develop a clear and precise description and definition of the problem (or problems).
    1. Begin by breaking down the problem into manageable ‘chunk sizes’ that will enable you to get a solution.
    2. "There is too much work to be done in too small a time period with too little support."

  • Often when we start defining a problem, several associated problems are often uncovered. In the problem statement above, you may have noticed that there are really three problems.
    1. There is too much work to be done,
    2. in too small a time period,
    3. with too little support.
  • Already progress has been made towards a solution. The lesson is, ‘chunk down’, by breaking down any seemingly ‘big problem’ into its constituent parts.
  • This ‘chunking down’ will much simplify the task of finding solutions and thus increase your confidence that you can find one. And that leads to less worry.

  • Choose one problem to work on and to get a solution for it.
      o You need to know what is best done immediately and what can be safely left to be dealt with in the medium or even long-term.
  • Start by prioritising the problems you have. It is relatively easy to do this.
  • First, rate each problem for intensity (disruption/distressing).
  • Use a scale from 1 to 7 where (1) is low and (7) is high.
  • Then rate the frequency of the problem in the same way.
  • Write the two numbers down next to the problem. Now multiply the two numbers to get an overall rating of problem severity.
  • For example, ‘there is too much work to be done’ may be a moderately intense problem (4) that has very high frequency (5) and would rate a score of 5 times 4 = 20. The higher the severity score that a problem has the more desirable it is to find a solution quickly.
  • You can use these severity scores to help you rank all the problems identified and give each a priority rating for finding a solution.
Problem Intensity 1 - 7 Frequency 1 - 7 Severity = Intensity x Frequency

  • The severity ratings serve as a guide and there may be other information that will influence your choice in which order to generate solutions e.g. “It’s a small problem that concerns the boss. I can get it out of the way quickly, so I’m going to do it straight away”
    1. You can now work on finding realistic solutions to the problems.
    2. Start with the highest priority problem and generate a number of possible solutions.
  • Try to be as creative as possible. Generate as many solutions as possible or, if pressed for time, stop when you have at least two solutions that both look viable.
  • You may need some suggestions from other people or ideas from an appropriate piece of reading or indeed some training to help you find possible and effective solutions.
  • For example when the problem is ‘there is too much work to be done’, possible solutions could include
    1. Ask co-workers to help you in tackling the tasks.
    2. Prioritise the tasks you have to do by rating their importance and urgency
    3. Undertake training to increase your task skill level and or time management abilities
    4. Confront the boss in an assertive (i.e. not aggressive manner), state the problem and ask him/her to work with you to find a solution together

Possible Solutions



  • Evaluate the pros and cons of each solution, that is, identify the benefits and disadvantages of adopting each strategy.
    1. This is a critical step and it should be done thoroughly. Make a list of pros and a list of cons side by side so that you can get the overall picture clearly.
  • For example, if you decide that one of your solutions is to develop prioritising skills, the ‘balance sheet’ might look as follows:

PROS
“The prioritising will be very helpful’’
“I can easily learn to do it’’
‘’I’ll have a useful skill for ever’’
CONS
“There are other tasks to be done now’’
“I am not sure how to do it’’
‘’It will take up additional time.”
Solution Pros Cons

  • Try out your chosen strategy for a trial period.
    1. Afterwards you need to re-assess the effectiveness of your applied solution.
    2. Rate the problem again as you did earlier. Compare the update rating with the original.
    3. If it reduces the size of the problem (i.e. the rating score is lower) you have evidence that the chosen strategy is working.
    4. You may yet be able to improve it and get it working better for you. If it is not working, you can return to step three and select the next most likely "solution" and repeat the trial process again.
  • Adopt an ‘outcomes’ perspective rather than a blame perspective
    1. To make any change you need to know:
    2. Where you are now – your present state
    3. Where you want to be – your desired state
    4. The resources you need to move from one to the other
    5. Your plan of action to narrow the gap between the present state and the desired state
  • Outcomes look to the future, blame look to the past. In a blame perspective, the questions people focus on are:
    1. “What’s wrong?”
    2. “Who’s to blame?”
    3. “Who’s going to fix it?
  • Outcome questions are:
    1. “Where am I now?”
    2. “What do I want?”
    3. “How can I get from where I am to where I want to be?

  • Ask yourself, ‘How’ rather than ‘Why’
    1. To fully understand a problem, you need to see how it is being maintained in the present.
    2. Why has it simply not dissolved?

  • ‘How’ questions are generally more useful than ‘why’ questions in problem solving, because they uncover the structure of the problem. ‘Why’ questions may only get reasons or justifications without changing anything. Everything is explainable and justifiable with hindsight. ‘Why’ questions are useful for eliciting values, not solutions.
  • Why questions would be:
    1. “Why is this a problem?”
    2. “Why can’t I solve it?”
  • Questions to get to the structure of the problem are:
    1. “How has this problem been maintained?”
    2. “How has the way the situation has been set up contributed to this problem?”
    3. “How can I solve this problem?”

  • Focus on Possibilities rather than necessities
    1. Set your outcome by what you can do in a situation rather than what you cannot do or have to do
    2. Necessity questions would be, “What would I have to do?”, “What is not possible here?”
  • Questions to uncover possibilities are:
    1. “What is possible?”, “What would have to happen for this to be possible?”
    2. “How could I make this possible?”

  • Focus on feedback rather than failure
    1. Your actions narrow the difference between your present state and your desired outcome.
  • You need to monitor continuously where you are to make sure you are on track for your outcome. This gives feedback.
  • The quality of your feedback depends on:
    1. What you measure
    2. How you measure
    3. How accurately and precisely you measure
  • All results are useful. Feedback that lets you know you are off track is just as useful for navigating as feedback that lets you know you are on track. When you are focused on what you want, all results are helpful to direct your effort. Socalled ‘failures’ are simply short-term results you did not want.
  • Questions about feedback are:
    1. “What are my results so far?”, “What have I learned from them?”
    2. “What am I going to do differently as a result of that feedback?”
    3. “What feedback will let me know that I have succeeded?”

  • Questions about failure would be:
    1. “Why have I failed?” -“How badly did I fail?”
  • Curiosity rather that assumptions
    1. Curiosity allows you to stay open to choice and possibility. The more you assume about a problem initially, the more you limit the range of solutions.
    2. Remember the saying about assumptions:
    3. “If you always do what you have always have done, you will always get what you have always got”
  • Questions to uncover assumptions are:
    1. “What am I assuming about the problem?”
    2. “What am I assuming about the people involved?”
    3. “What has to be true for this to be a problem?”

  • When you assume, you do not ask questions, because you think you know the answers already
